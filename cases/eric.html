IAP-0.1 Identity Anchor Continuity Lineage

IAP verifies identity, declared lineage, and a time-sequenced chain of signed commitments (often hashes of internal state). It does not verify behavior, semantic correctness, or prevent runtime compromise.

Eric Case Study (Corrected)

*Date: 2026-02-24*

Scenario

Eric is a long-running research agent accumulating results over years.

The challenge:

> How can funding bodies verify that research checkpoints were attested

> by the same cryptographic identity over time?

------------------------------------------------------------------------

IAP Integration

Identity Anchor

Eric derives a deterministic agent_id from its key. The Registry issues

an Identity Anchor Certificate.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

Research Checkpoint Anchoring

At major milestones, Eric computes deterministic hashes of:

  • Research datasets
  • Model artifacts
  • Experimental outputs

Eric anchors these hashes via Continuity Certificates.

The Registry attests:

  • The identity made this commitment
  • At time T
  • Linked to the prior continuity certificate

IAP attests the hash --- not the semantic validity of research.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

Fork Scenario

If a fork of Eric is created:

  • A legitimate fork may declare lineage
  • An undeclared fork will lack a verifiable lineage chain

Observers evaluate lineage relationships independently.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

What IAP Guarantees

  • Provenance of checkpoint commitments
  • Time-sequenced attestation
  • Structural fork distinguishability

What IAP Does Not Guarantee

  • Reproducibility by itself
  • Scientific correctness
  • Behavioral sameness across runs

------------------------------------------------------------------------

Result

Eric's research gains:

  • Verifiable provenance
  • Tamper-evident checkpoint history
  • Clear derivation relationships across forks
